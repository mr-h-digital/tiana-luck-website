How Fog Inspired Before Dawn

I wrote the first paragraph of Before Dawn at about 2am during a power cut. My phone was dying, I had a candle on my desk, and outside my window there was actual fog rolling in off the mountains. No joke.

I had this image in my head of a girl driving alone at night and everything around her slowly becoming unreliable — the GPS, the car, her phone, even the road itself. The fog felt like the perfect symbol for that: something that doesn't attack you, it just takes things away. One by one. Until you can't see where you are anymore.

The Rules Came First

Here's something most people don't know: I wrote the rules before I wrote the story. I started with those three instructions — don't go out after dawn, lock your windows, don't answer three slow knocks — and then worked backwards to figure out what kind of world would have rules like that.

That's actually a technique I love for supernatural fiction. Give the reader the shape of the threat before they see it. What do the rules protect against? The reader's brain will imagine something scarier than anything you describe directly.

"The most terrifying things are the ones that stay just out of view."

Writing Flora

Flora is my favourite character to write, partly because he makes no sense in the best possible way. He's calm when he should be afraid, warm when the situation calls for distance, and evasive in a way that feels protective rather than cruel. I wanted him to feel like he'd lived inside this secret for so long that it had become normal to him — and watching Yuki experience it fresh is a strange kind of grief for him.

More on that in a future post! But if you've read Chapter 3, you might have noticed that he was shaking after the bathroom scene. That wasn't a coincidence. That was a clue.

Until next time — stay curious, stay weird, and watch the fog. — T
